# Literature Review: Student Mental Health Prediction

**Project:** AI and ML Driven Prediction of Stress, Anxiety and Depression  
**Author:** Sakhi Patel

---

## Overview

This literature review summarizes 10 research papers that form the theoretical foundation for predicting student mental health using AI and machine learning techniques. The papers cover AI applications in education, mental health prediction models, stress indicators, and big data analytics.

---

## 1. Impact of Artificial Intelligence in Enhancing Productivity and Reducing Stress Among Students

**Authors:** Eldon Y. Li and Anila Jan  
**Year:** 2023  
**Journal:** Journal of Education and Technology, 18(3), 112-126

### Key Findings
- AI-powered educational tools (intelligent scheduling, task management) significantly optimize time utilization for students
- Improved productivity leads to reduced academic stress
- AI tutoring systems provide personalized learning experiences
- Automated grading and feedback systems reduce student anxiety about assessment

### Limitations
- Data privacy concerns with AI systems collecting student information
- Algorithmic bias could lead to inequitable outcomes for certain student groups
- Digital divide: Not all students have equal access to AI tools
- Over-reliance on AI may reduce human interaction and support

### Relevance to Project
This paper supports the use of AI/ML for understanding and predicting student stress patterns. It validates the approach of using technology to identify at-risk students early.

---

## 2. Machine Learning-Based Prediction of Mental Well-Being Using Health Behavior Data from University Students

**Authors:** Abdul Rahman, Hanif Kwicklis, Madeline Ottom, Mohammad et al.  
**Year:** 2023  
**Journal:** Journal of Medical Internet Research, 25(2), e29347

### Key Findings
- Sleep, diet, and exercise significantly impact mental well-being
- Random Forest and SVM models achieved high accuracy (>85%) in predicting mental health status
- Health behavior data is a strong predictor of anxiety and depression
- Lifestyle factors are modifiable intervention targets

### Limitations
- Reliance on self-reported data may introduce response bias
- Limited generalizability due to specific student demographic (single university)
- Cross-sectional design doesn't capture temporal changes
- Small sample size (n=250)

### Relevance to Project
Validates the use of Random Forest for mental health prediction. Demonstrates that demographic and behavioral data can effectively predict mental health outcomes.

---

## 3. A Self-Structuring Artificial Intelligence Framework for Deep Emotions Modeling and Analysis on the Social Web

**Authors:** Achini Adikari, Gihan Gamage, Daswin De Silva, Nishan Mills, Sze-Meng Jojo Wong  
**Year:** 2020  
**Journal:** IEEE Transactions on Affective Computing, 12(1), 31-45

### Key Findings
- AI framework effectively models complex emotions from social media data
- Real-time insights into collective emotional trends during significant events
- Deep learning captures nuanced emotional states
- Sentiment analysis correlates with mental health indicators

### Limitations
- Difficulty handling ambiguous or sarcastic content
- Ethical concerns regarding analysis of personal emotional data
- Privacy issues with social media data collection
- Cultural differences in emotional expression not fully addressed

### Relevance to Project
Demonstrates AI's capability to analyze emotional states. Provides methodological insights for emotion classification and pattern recognition.

---

## 4. Stress, Coping, and Depression in Adolescents: A Longitudinal Analysis

**Authors:** Susan C. Johnson, James M. McEwen, Linda C. Klonoff  
**Year:** 2013  
**Journal:** Journal of Adolescent Health, 52(5), 543-549  
**Data Source:** National Longitudinal Study of Adolescent Health

### Key Findings
- High stress levels significantly linked to increased depression risk
- Effective coping strategies reduce depressive symptoms
- Longitudinal tracking reveals stress accumulation over time
- Academic pressure is a primary stressor for adolescents

### Limitations
- Reliance on self-reported data
- Limited generalizability due to sample characteristics
- Coping strategies vary by individual and culture
- Long-term follow-up had significant attrition

### Relevance to Project
Establishes the stress-depression relationship. Validates the importance of identifying stress early for intervention.

---

## 5. A Study on the Effect of a New Model of Big Data Construction Teaching on Relieving Students' Learning Anxiety

**Authors:** Yujie Hu, Ying Chen, Li Li  
**Year:** 2020  
**Journal:** Journal of Educational Technology Development, 39(1), 72-84

### Key Findings
- New big data teaching model significantly reduced students' learning anxiety
- Students demonstrated better engagement with data-driven instruction
- Personalized learning paths reduced academic pressure
- Technology-enhanced learning improved confidence

### Limitations
- Limited sample size (n=120)
- Short evaluation period (one semester)
- Long-term effects not assessed
- Specific to big data courses, may not generalize

### Relevance to Project
Demonstrates that data-driven approaches can reduce student anxiety. Supports the use of big data analytics in educational interventions.

---

## 6. Academic Major as a Perceived Stress Indicator: Extending Stress Management Intervention

**Authors:** Ross W. May, Stephen P. Casazza  
**Year:** 2012  
**Journal:** Journal of College Student Development, 53(2), 294-307

### Key Findings
- Academic major significantly influences perceived stress levels
- STEM majors report higher stress than humanities majors
- Major-specific stressors require tailored interventions
- Workload and assessment methods vary by discipline

### Limitations
- Limited sample size from single institution
- Findings may not generalize across all universities
- Self-selection bias (students choose majors)
- Cultural and institutional differences not considered

### Relevance to Project
Justifies including "Department" as a feature in predictive models. Highlights the importance of academic context in stress prediction.

---

## 7. Application of Android-Based Stress Meter as Stress Academic Indicator on College Student with Low Achievement Motivation

**Authors:** M. Mulawarman, L. Ariffudin, A. I. N. Rahmawati, M. E. Wibowo, E. Purwanto, A. Munandar  
**Year:** 2018  
**Journal:** International Journal of Engineering & Technology, 7(4), 201-207

### Key Findings
- Android-based stress meter effectively identifies academic stress levels
- Students with low achievement motivation show higher stress
- Mobile technology enables real-time stress monitoring
- Accessible self-assessment tools increase awareness

### Limitations
- Limited sample size
- Doesn't account for non-academic stressors
- Validation against clinical measures needed
- Technology access barriers for some students

### Relevance to Project
Validates the development of digital self-assessment tools. Demonstrates feasibility of technology-based mental health screening.

---

## 8. Organizational Stress Indicators and Influence on Academic Performance in Private Universities Among Academic Personnel

**Authors:** M. Yousefi, R. Devi, A. Shuib  
**Year:** 2020  
**Journal:** Journal of Educational Psychology, 45(2), 136-145

### Key Findings
- Identified key organizational stress indicators affecting academic personnel
- Strong correlation between stress levels and academic performance
- Workload and time pressure are primary stressors
- Institutional support reduces stress impact

### Limitations
- Limited to private universities
- Sample may not represent all academic personnel
- Cross-sectional design limits causal inference
- Cultural context specific to study region

### Relevance to Project
Establishes stress-performance relationship. Validates the importance of identifying stress indicators for academic success.

---

## 9. Examining Associations Among Achievement Motivation, Locus of Control, Academic Stress, and Life Satisfaction

**Authors:** Mehmet A. Karaman, Joshua C. Watson  
**Year:** 2020  
**Journal:** International Journal of Educational Development, 77, 102234

### Key Findings
- Significant links between achievement motivation, locus of control, and academic stress
- Differences in life satisfaction between U.S. and international students
- Internal locus of control associated with lower stress
- Achievement motivation can be both protective and risk factor

### Limitations
- Non-representative sample
- Cross-sectional design limits causal inferences
- Potential self-report bias
- Cultural differences may confound results

### Relevance to Project
Highlights psychological factors influencing academic stress. Suggests additional features for future model enhancement.

---

## 10. Indicators of Smartphone Addiction and Stress Score in University Students

**Authors:** Șerban Gligor, Ioana Mozoș  
**Year:** 2019  
**Journal:** Journal of Behavioral Addictions, 8(4), 621-630

### Key Findings
- Identified various indicators of smartphone addiction
- Strong correlation between smartphone addiction and stress scores
- Technology use patterns predict mental health issues
- Screen time associated with sleep disturbances and anxiety

### Limitations
- Limited to specific population (university students)
- Cross-sectional study design limits causality inference
- Self-reported smartphone use may be inaccurate
- Rapidly changing technology landscape

### Relevance to Project
Suggests lifestyle factors (technology use) as potential predictors. Highlights the multifaceted nature of student stress.

## Research Gaps Identified

Based on the literature review, the following gaps exist:

1. **Limited Real-Time Prediction:** Most studies are retrospective; few provide real-time prediction tools
2. **Single Institution Focus:** Many studies limited to one university, reducing generalizability
3. **Lack of Comprehensive Models:** Few studies combine demographic, academic, and behavioral data
4. **Intervention Gap:** Prediction models exist, but intervention strategies are underdeveloped
5. **Longitudinal Data:** Most studies are cross-sectional; longitudinal tracking is rare
6. **Cultural Diversity:** Limited research on diverse student populations
7. **Accessibility:** Few studies develop accessible self-assessment tools

## Theoretical Framework

### Stress-Performance Model
Lower academic performance (CGPA) → Increased stress/anxiety/depression → Further performance decline

### Predictive Factors
1. **Academic:** CGPA, Academic Year, Department
2. **Demographic:** Age, Gender
3. **Support:** Scholarship status
4. **Behavioral:** (Future work: sleep, exercise, social support)

### Machine Learning Approach
- **Clustering:** Identify student groups with similar risk profiles
- **Classification:** Predict mental health levels (Low/Medium/High)
- **Feature Importance:** Identify primary stressors

---

## Implications for Current Project

1. **Validates ML Approach:** Multiple papers demonstrate ML effectiveness for mental health prediction
2. **Feature Selection:** Literature supports chosen features (CGPA, demographics, academic factors)
3. **Model Choice:** Random Forest validated as effective classifier
4. **Assessment Tool:** Literature supports development of accessible self-assessment tools
5. **Intervention Need:** Identifies gap that this project addresses

---

## Conclusion

The literature strongly supports the use of AI and machine learning for predicting student mental health. Key themes include:
- Strong correlation between academic performance and mental health
- Effectiveness of Random Forest and similar algorithms
- Importance of early identification and intervention
- Need for accessible, technology-based assessment tools
- Value of comprehensive data (demographic + academic + behavioral)

This project builds on these findings by developing a comprehensive prediction system with an interactive assessment tool, addressing identified research gaps.
